Rav Shmuel Berenstein

1773 - 1838

Biography

Rav Berenstein was born in Hannover (Germany) in a family of Rabonim and talmidei chachomim. His father was Rav Dov Berish, the chief Rabbi of Hannover and the grandson of Rav Yakov Yehoshua Pollack; the Pnei Yehoshua.

His Rebbezen was Rivka Reizel, the daughter of Rav Yakov Moshe Löwenstamm (son of the Binyan Ariel).

In 1802 Rav Berenstein became Rav of Groningen in the north of the Netherlands and in 1808 he was appointed as Rav in Friesland also in the north. Then, in 1811, Louis Bonaparte, the brother of Napoleon Bonaparte appointed him as leader of the Jewish Consistory of Holland and therefore he had to move to Amsterdam.

When in 1815 his father in law Rav Yakov Moshe was nifter, Rav Berenstein took over his position as Rav of Amsterdam at the same time taking upon himself the responsibility of the kehilla of Amersfoort.

He was one of the first Rabbonim in the Netherlands to give droshes in Dutch; some of them have been printed and published, like the droshe delivered in 1805 to arouse sympathy for those who were ruined by the great fire that almost destroyed the city of Leiden. This droshe has been translated in Hebrew and published in Hameassef (1809). He became well known as a great orator, in the jewish world as well in the non-jewish world. King Willem I used to read his droshes and visit the shul.

Rav Berenstein also became known for his strong stand against the reform movement. (see Hameasef 5669). In a teshuve from the Chasam Sofer (Even Haezer siman 139) we can see how highly he was regarded by him. The Chasam Sofer calls Rav Shmuel: “Geon Yisroel”, “Ner Yisroel”, “Ammud haYemani”, “Patish haChazak”, Kevod Kedosh Tifarto”.

Major Works

The hesped that Rav Berenstein held for Rav Chaim miVolozhin has been printed in Yeshurun (5667).

In 1832 Rav Berenstein published a Seder haTefillos to be used for davening for protection from a raging Cholera pandemic affecting the Dutch community. This pandemic was considered to have the highest fatalities of the 19th-century epidemics.
